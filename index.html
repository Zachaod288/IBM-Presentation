Company History

IBM has been a staple in technology for the past century and to this day retains a globally recognisable status. IBM has consistently produced products which have been considered a first of a kind and are regarded as being pioneers in the information industry. The inception of The International Time Recording Company (ITR) in 1889 marked the first step in IBM’s eventual global takeover with its introduction of the time recording clocks to manage employee shift times. After this, the founders of ITR and the Computing Tabulating Recording Company (CTR) realised the potential of using these machines to record more than just employees, but rather store data on companies products, record sales, and other uses. From the realising of the potential of storing vast amounts of data with machines, the information industry was born and IBM was founded in 1911. Since then, IBM has consistently produced both hardware and software with the capability of solving distinct and unique problems decade after decade. For example, in 1952 the IBM 709 computer was lauched, which was the first mass-produced vacuum computer. Following its predecessor, the IBM 7090 was introduced in 1959 which was one of the first fully transistorised mainframes and was capable of performing 229,000 calculations per second.

Recent Developments

In recent years, IBM has expanded their area of expertise by investing in researching new technologies. For example, IBM has developed an Artificial Intelligence persona, Watson, to undertake an assortment of different functions such as Speech to Text, Language Translator, Advertising Conversations, and many more. With a staggering 70% of global banking institutions using Watson, it shows artificial intelligence has become exceedingly prominent in industry over recent years and research into this field is still in its early stages. The potential for artificial intelligence to flourish is immense and at IBM, this potential is becoming a reality.

IBM has also developed IBM Q and the Quantum network, which is a recent innovation that allows developers to access qubits via cloud technology making it accessible to the public. This is also aided by its stylish presentation and added features such as showing the real and imaginary parts in the quantum bell state, and visualising both the probabilities and Bloch sphere of the system.
